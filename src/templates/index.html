The weight of a wall

Voices from the edge of Texas wrestle with ‘a big, beautiful, powerful wall’ through the borderland

By Angela Kocherga | Staff Writer
Kris Gutierrez and Maria Guerrero | NBC 5
Norma Garcia | Telemundo 39

Published May 17, 2017

    Satellite imagery: USGS/Landsat 8

    Early in 2017, The Dallas Morning News, KXAS-TV (NBC5) and Telemundo 39 gathered a team of writers and photojournalists to document the federal government’s effort to construct a “physically imposing” and “aesthetically pleasing” 30-foot concrete structure.

    The “Borderland Project” is the result of that commitment. Our stories trace the border from the deserts near El Paso to the fertile valley at the tip of Texas. Most of that land is divided by the natural boundary of the Rio Grande. The river carves a path through diverse landscapes and cityscapes that reflect the rich history of both Texas and Mexico. Each stop along our trip reveals a new set of voices grappling with the tangle of issues along one of the busiest international boundaries in the world, and we’ll continue to add more voices throughout the year.

    Near Hachita, NM
    31.790043, -107.948805

    The front line

    Camp Ramsey sits on the New Mexico-Mexico border, in the middle of a vast, wide-open desert that includes much of the United States’ southern border. It serves as a forward operating base for U.S. Customs and Border Protection, 130 miles from El Paso, where Border Patrol agents operate for eight-day stretches at a time. The camp is a means of putting resources closer to the border, where vigilant agents know that there is always someone trying to get in.

    El Paso borderland
    31.752576, -106.487386

    In west Texas, hurdles abound

    In the vast western reaches of Texas, the border wall faces many challenges. Private landowners balk at having the barrier on their land. Locals view a border wall as an idea conceived by those who don’t know or understand the region. The city of El Paso would rather spend the money on more bridges to help alleviate traffic during peak crossing times. And Reps. Will Hurd, R-San Antonio, and Beto O’Rourke, D-El Paso, both oppose the idea of a wall, calling it the most expensive and least effective way to do border security. Read more.

    Presidio
    29.562273, -104.376493

    Neighbors across the border

    The tiny border town of Presidio sits in the Big Bend region of West Texas, more than 200 miles from major cities like Midland-Odessa or El Paso. Its closest neighbor, Ojinaga, is five times the size and just across the Rio Grande in Mexico. The two communities, whose bloodlines and economic ties extend across the border, fear a wall will hamper a relationship in which the two communities rely on each other both economically and in times of emergency. Read more.

    Lake Amistad, near Del Rio
    29.449884, -101.058174

    Life on the Lake

    For some, the words “border wall” conjure images of a dusty landscape with a fence following the trickle of the Rio Grande through the West Texas desert. But for the people of Val Verde County, the border wall is something else. It’s a question: How do you get a border wall through the rocky landscape of one of the state’s premier fishing spots? Lake Amistad and the city of Del Rio are about 150 miles west of San Antonio, but they’re at the center of a heated discussion about the Mexican border and security.

    Laredo
    27.597660, -99.536872

    Questions of commerce

    Laredo occupies a unique spot in the borderland. The city’s geography and history have long made it a focal point for commercial trade. In 2015, more than $260 billion in commercial trade flowed through the city. The Bureau of Transportation Statistics lists Laredo as the largest point of entry for loaded trucks along the southern border. We were given a unique look at how Customs and Border Protection agents keep people and goods trucking across the bridges of Laredo.

    Brooks County, Texas
    -98.193096, 27.090503

    Volunteer eyes and ears

    Eighty miles north of the Texas-Mexico border in Brooks County, South Texas rancher Mike Vickers leads the Texas Border Volunteers, a group of nearly 300 men and women who gather monthly to conduct undercover operations along the border. Their mission is to report criminal trespassing by unauthorized immigrants crossing into the U.S. Some think they are just vigilantes, but multiple law enforcement agencies across South Texas rely on them as an extra set of eyes and ears to help the Border Patrol.

    McAllen-Reynosa border
    26.120063, -98.292448

    The cost of securing the borderland

    The Rio Grande is a 1,200-mile-long first line of defense across Texas’ southern border, and Texas Department of Public Safety troopers will tell you it costs a lot to defend. Troopers use boats outfitted with Kevlar panels and .30-caliber machine guns, as well as helicopters and thousands of cameras to monitor illegal crossings. According to Texas lawmakers, that’s money well-spent, and the number of unauthorized immigrant apprehensions backs up that claim.

    Brownsville
    25.9011389, -97.5000273

    The artist’s view

    Not far from where the Rio Grande meets the Gulf of Mexico sits the city of Brownsville, home to Mark Clark. Clark retired to Brownsville to embrace his love of painting. But now his inspiration, the view of the Rio Grande, has been ruined by the border fence. Clark has abandoned landscape paintings in favor of satire as a way to protest what he calls an obscene gesture to our neighbors across the river.
